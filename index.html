PART 0: The Big Picture

Foundation

What is This Course About?

Applied Econometrics II answers ONE fundamental question: Does X cause Y?

In Spanish: ¿Causa X a Y?

This is harder than it seems. Observing that Y goes up when X goes up does NOT prove causation. There could be:

  • Confounding variables (Z causes both X and Y)
  • Reverse causality (Y causes X)
  • Selection bias (people who choose X are different)

The Fundamental Problem of Causal Inference

For each unit (person, firm, etc.), there are TWO potential outcomes:

Y(1) = outcome if treated Y(0) = outcome if not treated

The individual treatment effect is: τᵢ = Y(1) - Y(0)

The problem: We can NEVER observe both Y(1) and Y(0) for the same unit. You either get the treatment or you don't. This is why we need causal inference methods.

In Spanish: No podemos ver ambos resultados potenciales para la misma persona.

The Switching Equation (CRITICAL)

Yᵢ = Dᵢ·Y(1) + (1-Dᵢ)·Y(0)

Where:

  • Yᵢ = observed outcome
  • Dᵢ = treatment indicator (1 if treated, 0 if control)
  • Y(1) = potential outcome if treated
  • Y(0) = potential outcome if not treated

This just says: you observe Y(1) if you're treated, Y(0) if you're not.

TRAP: D=1 Does NOT Mean Y(0)=0

Many students think: "If treatment is given (D=1), then Y(0) must be 0 or doesn't exist."

WRONG! Y(0) is a potential outcome. It's the outcome that would have happened if that person had NOT been treated. It exists conceptually even if we never observe it.

Example: If a student takes tutoring (D=1), Y(0) is their grade IF they didn't take tutoring. This is real and meaningful, even though we never see it.

Why These Methods Matter for the Exam

The quiz tests your ability to distinguish between:

  • Observed differences (SDO): E[Y|D=1] - E[Y|D=0]
  • True causal effects (ATE, LATE): different ways of identifying τ
  • Selection bias: why observed ≠ causal

Each method (RCT, DiD, IV) solves the fundamental problem in different ways.

PART 1: The Rubin Causal Model

Parameters & Assumptions

What Are We Trying to Estimate?

We want to measure the causal effect of treatment. But there are different "types" of effects, depending on whose effect we care about.

All depend on the question: Effect for whom?

1A. CAUSAL PARAMETERS (What We Estimate)

Average Treatment Effect (ATE)

ATE = E[Y(1) - Y(0)]

The average effect for everyone in the population.

In Spanish: El efecto promedio para toda la población

Average Treatment Effect on the Treated (ATT)

ATT = E[Y(1) - Y(0) | D=1]

The average effect only for people who actually got treated.

This matters because people who self-select into treatment might be different.

Average Treatment Effect on the Untreated (ATU)

ATU = E[Y(1) - Y(0) | D=0]

The average effect for people who didn't get treated.

This is often different from ATT, especially if treatment effects are heterogeneous.

Relationship Between ATE, ATT, and ATU

ATE = p·ATT + (1-p)·ATU

Where p = P(D=1) = share of population treated.

This is just a weighted average. If treatment is given to everyone, p=1 so ATE=ATT. If given to half, then ATE is the average of ATT and ATU.

1B. OBSERVED vs CAUSAL: The SDO Decomposition (CRITICAL)

Simple Difference in Outcomes (SDO)

SDO = E[Y | D=1] - E[Y | D=0]

This is what you see in the data: average outcome for treated minus average for control.

In Spanish: La diferencia simple observada en los datos

The Problem with SDO

SDO includes BOTH:

  • The true causal effect (what we want)
  • Selection bias (differences between treated and control groups)

So: SDO ≠ ATE unless there's no selection bias.

SDO Decomposition: Version 1

SDO = ATT + Selection Bias where Selection Bias = E[Y(0)|D=1] - E[Y(0)|D=0]

Selection Bias tells you: How much would the treated group's outcome have differed from the control group, even without treatment?

This captures differences in Y(0) between groups.

Worked Example from PS1 (Non-Random Case)

Data: Some people self-selected into treatment, some didn't.

  • SDO = 10.22
  • ATT = 10.27
  • ATU = 5.49
  • Selection Bias = -0.051

Interpretation: The treated group's observed outcome was 10.22 higher than control. But ATT is only 10.27, which is very close! This is because Selection Bias ≈ 0 (despite non-random selection). The treated group would have been only 0.051 higher even without treatment, so most of the SDO is real causal effect.

Worked Example from PS1 (Random Case)

Data: Treatment was randomly assigned.

  • SDO = 8.16
  • ATE = 7.88
  • ATT = 8.16
  • ATU = 7.60
  • Selection Bias = -0.006

Interpretation: Randomization makes SB ≈ 0 (barely perceptible -0.006). This is much better than the non-random case. With randomization, SDO ≈ ATE (8.16 vs 7.88), and ATT ≈ ATU, showing no heterogeneity.

SDO Decomposition: Version 2 (Heterogeneous Treatment Effects)

SDO = ATE + SB + (1-p)·(ATT - ATU)

This breaks SDO into three parts:

  • ATE: the true average effect
  • Selection Bias (SB): composition differences
  • Heterogeneous Treatment Effect (HTE) bias: (1-p)·(ATT-ATU), which arises if treatment effects differ between treated and untreated

In the examples above: from PS1, HTE = (ATT - ATU) = 10.27 - 5.49 = 4.78 in non-random case, 0.56 in random case.

Key Insight: Why Randomization Helps

Randomization solves the problem by making:

  • Selection Bias ≈ 0 (groups are balanced on Y(0))
  • HTE bias ≈ 0 (no reason for effect to differ by D)

So: With randomization, SDO ≈ ATE.

1C. Independence Assumption

The Independence Assumption

(Y(1), Y(0)) ⊥⊥ D

This says: Treatment assignment is independent of potential outcomes.

In plain English: whether you get treated or not is uncorrelated with how you would respond to treatment.

What Independence Means for SDO and ATE

If independence holds:

E[Y(0)|D=1] = E[Y(0)|D=0] → Selection Bias = 0 → SDO = ATE

This is because: if treatment is independent of potential outcomes, the treated group's counterfactual (Y(0)) should be the same as the control group's Y(0).

TRAP: Independence ≠ No Effect

Independence does NOT mean SDO = 0 or ATE = 0. It means SDO = ATE (unbiased estimate).

Example: If ATE = 5, then SDO = 5 (not 0). The effect is real; it's just correctly estimated.

How to Achieve Independence

Randomization is the gold standard. When you randomly assign treatment, it becomes independent of potential outcomes by design.

1D. SUTVA (Stable Unit Treatment Value Assumption)

SUTVA Requirements

Two parts:

  1. No interference: One person's treatment doesn't affect another person's outcome
  2. Single version of treatment: Treatment means the same thing for everyone

SUTVA Violations

  • Vaccines: If one person gets vaccinated, it protects others (herd immunity). SUTVA violated.
  • Job training: If some people get training, wages might fall for others (labor market effects). SUTVA violated.
  • Classroom size: If one class is smaller, does it affect learning in other classes? If yes, SUTVA violated.
  • Drug dosage variation: If "treatment" means 5mg for some and 10mg for others, single version violated.

What Happens If SUTVA Is Violated?

Your causal effect estimates become unreliable. You might estimate the effect of the direct treatment, but miss spillovers to other units.

Later in Part 3, we'll cover saturation designs that handle interference.

1E. Selection Bias Direction: How to Reason Through It

The Key Question: Why Do People Self-Select?

In observational data, selection bias arises because people choose treatment based on how much they'll benefit.

From PS1 Q3d: Ask yourself—are treated people inherently higher-Y or lower-Y than controls?

Worked Example: College Education and Earnings

SDO: College grads earn $15k more than non-grads.

But: Smarter/more motivated people self-select into college. They'd earn more anyway (higher Y(0)).

So: SB > 0 (treated have higher Y(0)).

Conclusion: SDO overestimates ATE. True effect is less than $15k.

Counter-Example: Job Training for Unemployed

SDO: Job training participants earn $5k more 2 years later than non-participants.

But: People self-select into training because they're struggling (lower Y(0)).

So: SB < 0 (treated have lower Y(0)).

Conclusion: SDO underestimates ATE. True effect is more than $5k.

How to Answer Selection Bias Direction Questions

Step 1: Ask: Why would people choose treatment?

Step 2: If people with higher Y(0) self-select → SB > 0 → SDO overestimates

Step 3: If people with lower Y(0) self-select → SB < 0 → SDO underestimates

PART 2: Randomized Experiments (RCTs)

Design & Analysis

Why RCTs Are the Gold Standard

Randomization forces Independence: (Y(1), Y(0)) ⊥⊥ D. This means:

  • Selection Bias = 0
  • SDO = ATE (unbiased estimate of the true causal effect)
  • No confounding

RCTs are the most credible causal inference method.

2A. Basic RCT Regression Specification

Simplest RCT Regression

Yᵢ = β₀ + β₁·Treatᵢ + εᵢ

Under randomization: β̂₁ = ATE (unbiased)

β₀ = E[Y|D=0] = control group mean

β₀ + β₁ = E[Y|D=1] = treated group mean

With Multiple Follow-Up Periods

Yᵢₜ = β₀ + Σ βₜ·Treatᵢ·Tᵢₘₑₜ + λₜ + εᵢₜ

This allows treatment effects to vary over time.

Each βₜ estimates ATE in period t after treatment.

λₜ are time fixed effects (capture common trends).

Adding Controls (Pre-Treatment Variables)

Yᵢ = β₀ + β₁·Treatᵢ + Xᵢ'γ + εᵢ

Where X = pre-treatment characteristics (measured before randomization).

Why add controls? Reduces Var(εᵢ) → smaller SE → more power.

Key: Must be measured BEFORE treatment. Post-treatment variables are "bad controls" (they might be affected by treatment).

ANCOVA (Analysis of Covariance)

Yᵢ = β₀ + β₁·Treatᵢ + Y₀ᵢ'γ + εᵢ

Control for baseline outcome Y₀ᵢ (outcome measured before treatment).

Very powerful: controls for unit-specific factors that predict outcomes.

Reduces noise, increases precision of β̂₁.

Stratified Design (from class notes)

Yᵢ = β₀ + β₁·Treatᵢ + Sₛ + εᵢ

Sₛ = strata fixed effects (one for each pre-defined group).

Ensures treatment is balanced within each stratum, not just overall.

When to use: Expect heterogeneous effects across strata.

TRAP: Bad Controls in RCTs

Bad controls = variables measured AFTER treatment that might be affected by treatment.

Example: If treatment is tutoring, don't control for "study hours" if tutoring changes study behavior.

Why? You'd be controlling away part of the treatment effect!

From PS1: "wellbeing" measured post-treatment is a bad control.

2B. Why Include Controls in RCT?

The Precision Argument

Controls reduce Var(εᵢ) → SE(β̂₁) is smaller → can detect smaller effects → more power.

But: randomization ensures unbiased β̂₁ even without controls.

Pre-specification: Should plan which controls to use before analyzing data (AEA registry).

PDS/Lasso for Variable Selection

If you have many possible controls, you can use data-driven methods (like Lasso) to select which ones to include.

Modern best practice: pre-specify a few strong controls, then report robustness to Lasso selection.

2C. Stratification

Without Stratification

Random assignment is balanced in expectation, but in any given sample, you might get imbalance by chance.

Example: Want 50% treatment, but get 52% by random chance.

With Stratification

You ensure balance in practice by assigning treatment separately within pre-defined groups (strata).

Example: Stratify by gender, then randomly assign 50% within each gender. Guarantees 50% treated overall.

Benefit: Improves power, especially if stratification variable predicts outcomes.

Stratification Strategy (from notes)

Who to stratify by? Variables where you expect heterogeneous treatment effects.

How many strata? As many as feasible, up to 2×(number of treatment arms).

Example: 2 treatment arms, can safely stratify by ≤4 groups.

2D. Internal vs External Validity

Internal Validity

Can the estimate be interpreted as causal within this study?

RCTs have high internal validity because randomization eliminates confounding.

External Validity

Does the effect generalize to other populations/settings?

RCTs may have lower external validity if sample is selective (e.g., volunteers).

For the Exam

You might be asked: "This RCT is internally valid. Is it externally valid?" Answer: Probably not—depends on whether the sample represents the target population.

2E-F. NON-COMPLIANCE AND LATE (CRITICAL FOR EXAM)

The Non-Compliance Problem

In real experiments, not everyone assigned to treatment takes it. Not everyone in control stays untreated.

Example: Randomize job training. Some assigned don't attend. Some in control seek training elsewhere.

This complicates causal inference.

Key Notation

Z = assignment (exogenous, random) D = actual treatment received (endogenous, may depend on Z and unobservables)

We control Z (assignment), but D (compliance) is partly out of our control.

The Four Types of Compliance Behavior

Compliers: D = 1 if Z = 1, D = 0 if Z = 0 (follow assignment) Always-Takers: D = 1 regardless of Z Never-Takers: D = 0 regardless of Z Defiers: D = 0 if Z = 1, D = 1 if Z = 0 (do opposite)

Calculating Compliance Types (from PS1 Q2)

Data:

  • 65% assigned treatment (Z=1), 35% control (Z=0)
  • 77.54% of Z=1 participated (D=1|Z=1 = 0.7754)
  • 6% of Z=0 participated (D=1|Z=0 = 0.06)

Calculations:

Always-Takers = P(D=1|Z=0) = 6% Never-Takers = 1 - P(D=1|Z=1) = 1 - 0.7754 = 22.46% Compliers = P(D=1|Z=1) - P(D=1|Z=0) = 0.7754 - 0.06 = 0.7154 (71.54%)

Check: Always + Never + Compliers = 6% + 22.46% + 71.54% = 100%. ✓

ITT (Intention-to-Treat)

ITT = E[Y | Z=1] - E[Y | Z=0]

Key: Uses Z (assignment), not D (actual treatment).

ITT is the effect of being assigned to treatment, whether or not you comply.

ITT ≤ ATE because not everyone complies.

First Stage

First Stage = P(D=1|Z=1) - P(D=1|Z=0) = share of compliers

In the PS1 example: 0.7754 - 0.06 = 0.7154 = 71.54%

LATE (Local Average Treatment Effect)

LATE = ITT / First Stage

LATE is the effect of treatment for compliers only.

From PS1 Q2i: ITT = -1.1457, First Stage = 0.7154, LATE = -1.1457 / 0.7154 = -1.6015

Why |LATE| ≥ |ITT|?

LATE divides ITT by First Stage, which is < 1 (unless compliance is 100%).

So LATE is always larger in absolute value than ITT.

Intuition: If only 71.54% comply, then to get the effect for those 71.54%, we must divide by 0.7154 to "undo" the dilution.

From PS1 Q2j: Why |LATE| > |ITT|?

Answer: ITT includes all people assigned to treatment, many of whom didn't participate. LATE adjusts for non-compliance to estimate the effect for compliers only. Since many assigned didn't comply, we divide by a smaller fraction, making LATE larger.

When to Use ITT vs LATE

ITT: Use if you want the policy effect (effect of offering treatment, whether people take it or not).

LATE: Use if you want the treatment effect for those who actually take it.

2G. Four LATE Assumptions (CRITICAL)

Assumption 1: Independence

Z ⊥⊥ (Y, D potentials)

Assignment is random. Holds by design in RCTs.

Assumption 2: Exclusion Restriction

Z → Y only through D

Assignment affects outcomes only through actual treatment, not through other channels.

Example violation: If Z = job training offered, and showing up (D) signals motivation, then Z might affect outcomes through motivation independent of training. Bad.

Assumption 3: First Stage (Relevance)

P(D=1|Z=1) ≠ P(D=1|Z=0)

Assignment must actually affect whether people take treatment. If this doesn't hold, Z can't be used as an instrument.

In all real experiments with compliance, this holds.

Assumption 4: Monotonicity

No defiers: D(Z=1) ≥ D(Z=0) for all individuals

People assigned to treatment don't actively avoid it (relative to being assigned control).

Usually reasonable assumption in practice.

For Exam: Critiquing LATE

You might be asked: "Can we use this as an instrument?" Check all four assumptions. If one is violated (especially exclusion), LATE is biased.

2H. Adding Controls with LATE (from PS1 Q2k)

The Complication

When calculating LATE with controls, you need to be careful about what counts as a "bad control."

From PS1 Q2k: Don't include "wellbeing" as control because measured post-treatment.

Correct LATE with Controls

When using 2SLS with controls:

First Stage: D = π₀ + π₁·Z + X'φ + ν Second Stage: Y = β₀ + β₁·D̂ + X'γ + ε

Include the same X in both stages.

Key: X must be pre-treatment variables that predict outcomes.

2I. Balance Checks

What Are Balance Checks?

Verify that randomization was successful by checking if treatment and control groups are similar on pre-treatment characteristics.

Example: Is age distributed similarly in treatment vs control?

From PS1 Q3e

Create a balance table showing mean of each pre-treatment variable by treatment status. If means are close, randomization worked.

Formal test: t-test for each variable: H₀: mean(X|D=1) = mean(X|D=0)

Expect: Only ~5% of variables significantly different at p<0.05 (by chance).

Exam Tip

If asked about balance: "Check pre-treatment variables between groups. If similar, randomization worked and SDO = ATE."

2J. Randomization Inference

What Is It?

An alternative to asymptotic inference (t-tests). Use permutations of treatment assignment to build the distribution of the test statistic under the null.

More reliable with small samples.

Fisher's Sharp Null

H₀: τᵢ = 0 for ALL i (not just on average)

This is stronger than "E[τ] = 0"—it says the treatment has zero effect for every single person.

Procedure

  1. Calculate observed test statistic (e.g., difference in means)
  2. Permute treatment assignment R times (e.g., R=10,000)
  3. Calculate test statistic for each permutation
  4. p-value = share of permutations with stat ≥ observed

From PS1

10,000 permutations, p-value = 0 (observed effect more extreme than all permutations).

Conclusion: Strong evidence against Fisher's null (effect is real and nonzero).

2K. Spillovers & Saturation Design

The Problem: SUTVA Violations

If one person's treatment affects another person's outcome (spillovers), SUTVA is violated and standard estimates are biased.

Example: If some people get bednet training, those nearby benefit from reduced malaria. If some get treated and some nearby don't, we mix direct and spillover effects.

Saturation Design (Solution)

Randomize groups (villages, neighborhoods) instead of individuals.

Three treatment arms:

High Saturation (80% treated) Low Saturation (20% treated) Control (0% treated)

Regression with Saturation Design

Y = β₀ + β₁·Treat_High + β₂·Treat_Low + β₃·Control_High + β₄·Control_Low + ε

β₁ = Direct effect of treatment + spillover in high saturation

β₂ = Direct effect of treatment + spillover in low saturation

β₃ = Spillover effect on controls in high saturation (everyone else treated)

β₄ = Spillover effect on controls in low saturation (few others treated)

Interpreting Saturation Results

If β₁ ≠ β₂: spillovers are present (effect differs by saturation).

If β₃ ≠ 0: spillovers exist even to untreated in treated areas.

2L. Multiple Treatment Arms & Factorial Designs

3-Arm Design (Two Treatments)

Yᵢ = β₀ + β₁·Treat1ᵢ + β₂·Treat2ᵢ + εᵢ

β₁ = ATE of Treatment 1 vs control

β₂ = ATE of Treatment 2 vs control

Comparing Two Treatments

H₀: β₁ = β₂ (treatment effects are equal) Test: t-stat = (β̂₁ - β̂₂) / SE(β̂₁ - β̂₂)

2×2 Factorial Design (Two Binary Treatments)

Yᵢ = β₀ + β₁·T¹ᵢ + β₂·T²ᵢ + β₃·(T¹ᵢ · T²ᵢ) + εᵢ

β₁ = Main effect of Treatment 1 (holding Treatment 2 at 0)

β₂ = Main effect of Treatment 2 (holding Treatment 1 at 0)

β₃ = Interaction (additional effect when both given together)

Exam Question Example

You might see: "Test if treatment 1 and treatment 2 have the same effect." Use comparison in formula above.

PART 3: Difference-in-Differences (DiD)

Observable Designs

Why DiD When We Can't Randomize?

Often randomization is impossible (policy is mandatory, already implemented, etc.). DiD exploits natural experiments: some units get treated, others don't, and we observe both before and after.

Key insight: Parallel trends assumption replaces randomization.

3A. Basic DiD Setup and Decomposition

The 2×2 Table

Group Pre-Policy Post-Policy Change
Control C D D - C
Treated A B B - A
Diff-in-Diff A - C B - D (B - A) - (D - C)

Regression Specification

Yᵢₜ = β₀ + β₁·Dᵢ + β₂·Postₜ + β₃·(Dᵢ × Postₜ) + εᵢₜ

Interpretation:

  • β₀ = C (control, pre-policy mean)
  • β₁ = A - C (treatment group baseline difference)
  • β₂ = D - C (post-policy change in control group)
  • β₃ = DiD = (B - A) - (D - C) = causal effect (THE coefficient of interest)

Key Insight

β₃ is the ATT (effect for treated group). It's identified by comparing the trend in the treated group to the trend in the control group.

3B. Worked Example from PS2 Q2d (CRITICAL)

Air Pollution Policy and Accidents

Setup: Government implemented air pollution policy in some provinces but not others. Question: Did the policy reduce traffic accidents?

Data: Monthly accident counts, before and after policy implementation.

Accidentsᵢₜ = β₀ + β₁·Treatmentᵢ + β₂·Postₜ + β₃·(Treatmentᵢ × Postₜ) + εᵢₜ

Results from PS2 Q2d

  • β̂₀ = 67.33: Mean accidents in Control group, pre-policy
  • β̂₁ = 17.52: Treatment group had 17.52 MORE accidents pre-policy (bad luck, not causality)
  • β̂₂ = -2.54: Post-policy, control group had 2.54 fewer accidents (common trend)
  • β̂₃ = -5.34: DiD effect! Treatment group had 5.34 FEWER accidents due to policy

Calculation check:

  • Control, pre: 67.33
  • Control, post: 67.33 - 2.54 = 64.79
  • Treated, pre: 67.33 + 17.52 = 84.85
  • Treated, post: 67.33 + 17.52 - 2.54 - 5.34 = 76.97

DiD = (84.85 - 76.97) - (67.33 - 64.79) = 7.88 - 2.54 = 5.34 ✓

Interpretation

Bottom line: The policy reduced accidents by 5.34 per 100,000 per month in treated provinces.

Important: This is ATT, not ATE. Provinces that implemented policy may differ from those that didn't. If extending policy to other provinces, ATT might not equal ATE.

Clustering Standard Errors

From PS2: Cluster SE at province level (the treatment level). This accounts for correlation of errors within provinces over time.

Why? If accidents in one month for a province correlate with next month, regular SE is too small.

3C. Two-Way Fixed Effects (TWFE) Specification

TWFE with Unit and Time FE

Accidentsᵢₜ = β₀ + β₁·(Treatmentᵢ × Postₜ) + λᵢ + αₜ + εᵢₜ

λᵢ = unit (province) fixed effects. Absorb all time-invariant differences between units.

αₜ = time fixed effects. Absorb all common time shocks.

Why Use TWFE?

Eliminates need to include Treatmentᵢ standalone (absorbed by λᵢ) and Postₜ standalone (absorbed by αₜ).

β̂₁ = DiD effect (same as 2×2 case), but estimated more flexibly.

From PS2 Q2e

Results: β̂₁ = -5.34 (same as simple DiD).

Why? With just two time periods (pre and post), TWFE reduces to simple DiD.

With more periods: TWFE allows flexible time effects, which is more general.

ATT Interpretation

β̂₁ is still ATT. It's the effect on treated units relative to what they would have experienced following the control group's trend.

3D. Why Variables Don't Appear Standalone When Fixed Effects Included

From PS2 Q3a

Question: Why don't we include Electᵢₜ (election indicator) standalone when there are year fixed effects?

Answer: Because Electᵢₜ only varies by year (same value for all units in a given year). Year FE (αₜ) perfectly absorb this variation. Including both would be collinear.

Same for Treatmentᵢ standalone: Only varies by unit. Absorbed by unit FE (λᵢ).

Rule of Thumb

Don't include a variable standalone if it's already perfectly absorbed by your fixed effects. The regressor must vary within treatment/time cell.

3E. PARALLEL TRENDS ASSUMPTION (THE KEY ASSUMPTION)

Parallel Trends: Absent treatment, the treated and control groups would have followed the same trend over time.

Why Is This Crucial?

DiD uses the control group's trend as the counterfactual for the treated group. If trends differ for reasons unrelated to treatment, this breaks down.

Example: If treated province has economic growth independent of policy, its outcome would rise faster anyway. DiD would confound growth with policy effect.

What Parallel Trends Means Mathematically

Untreated Trend: E[Y(0)ᵢₜ₊₁ - Y(0)ᵢₜ | Dᵢ=1] = E[Y(0)ᵢₜ₊₁ - Y(0)ᵢₜ | Dᵢ=0]

Without treatment, treated group would grow at same rate as controls.

TRAP: Parallel Trends ≠ Parallel Levels

Groups CAN have different starting levels. β₁ ≠ 0 is fine.

Parallel trends only requires that changes are the same, not that levels are.

TRAP: Pre-Trends ≈ 0 ≠ Parallel Trends Hold

If both groups show zero trend pre-treatment, that's nice but doesn't prove parallel trends.

You need E[Y|Treat,Pre] to grow at same rate as E[Y|Control,Pre].

Testing Parallel Trends (Pre-Treatment Only)

You CAN test pre-treatment trends. If they're similar pre-treatment, it's suggestive evidence for parallel trends post-treatment.

Cannot test post-treatment (because treatment affects it!), so must assume it holds.

3F. Testing Parallel Pre-Trends

Visual Inspection (from PS2)

Plot E[Y|Treat,t] and E[Y|Control,t] for each time period before treatment.

If lines are parallel, parallel trends assumption seems reasonable.

Formal F-Test

H₀: β₁ = β₂ = ... = βₖ = 0 (all pre-treatment coefficients = 0) H_A: at least one pre-treatment β ≠ 0

If you can't reject H₀, no evidence against parallel pre-trends.

From PS2 Q2g

Data: F-test p-value = 0.7818 for pre-treatment coefficients.

Interpretation: Fail to reject H₀. No statistical evidence against parallel pre-trends.

Conclusion: Treated and control groups trended similarly before treatment. Good sign that parallel trends holds post-treatment.

Exam Tip

If asked "Does parallel trends hold?": Say "We cannot test post-treatment directly, but pre-treatment trends are [similar/different]. This is [weak/strong] suggestive evidence."

3G. EVENT STUDY SPECIFICATION (CRITICAL)

What Is an Event Study?

Instead of assuming treatment effects are constant over time, estimate separate effects for each period relative to treatment.

Shows whether effects are immediate, fade, grow, etc.

Event Study Regression (from PS2)

Yᵢₜ = β₀ + λᵢ + Σ γₜ·Dₜ + Σ βₜ·(Dₜ × Treatmentᵢ) + εᵢₜ

λᵢ = unit FE

γₜ = time FE (coefficient for each time period)

βₜ = interaction with treatment (period-specific treatment effect)

One period omitted (e.g., k=-1 or reference period 6) to avoid multicollinearity.

Interpreting γₜ (Time FE Coefficients, from PS2 Q1)

γₜ: Difference between control group's average outcome in period t and control group's average in the omitted (reference) period.

Shows how the control group evolves over time.

Interpreting βₜ (Treatment × Period Interactions)

βₜ: Difference between treated and control groups in period t, relative to the reference period.

This is the period-t DiD effect.

Pre-treatment βₜ should ≈ 0: If so, supports parallel trends.

Post-treatment βₜ: The treatment effects in each period.

From PS2 Q1e Results

Pre-policy periods: β̂ₜ ≈ 0 (consistent with parallel trends).

Post-policy: β̂ₜ initially negative (policy reduces accidents), then trend toward zero.

Interpretation: Policy has immediate effect that fades over time (possibly adaptation by drivers).

Why Event Study?

More flexible than assuming one constant effect. Shows dynamic response. Helps test parallel trends assumption (pre-trends should be ≈0).

3H. Additional DiD Topics

DiD with Repeated Cross-Sections (not panel)

DiD works even if you don't follow same units over time.

Example: Survey different people each year, but same geographic units.

Yᵢₜ = β₀ + β₁·Treatmentᵢ + β₂·Postₜ + β₃·(Treatmentᵢ × Postₜ) + εᵢₜ

Still works because what matters is area-level treatment, not individual tracking.

Clustering Standard Errors (from class)

Cluster at treatment assignment level (e.g., unit level in panel data).

Why: Errors are correlated within units over time.

Rule of thumb: Need ≥30 clusters for clustering to work reliably.

Continuous Treatment Intensity

Treatment doesn't have to be binary. Can be dose/intensity.

Yᵢₜ = β₀ + β₁·(Doseᵢ × Postₜ) + ... + εᵢₜ

β₁ interprets as effect per unit increase in dose.

Robustness Checks (from PS2)

Falsification tests: Show no effect on subgroups that shouldn't be affected by treatment.

Placebo tests: Show null effect on outcomes unrelated to treatment (e.g., bednet → broken bones).

These strengthen credibility of causal interpretation.

3I. Staggered Rollout / Adoption (Advanced)

The Complication

What if treatment rolls out at different times to different groups? Event study is still OK, but some additional caveats apply.

When Is Staggered Rollout OK?

Event study is valid if:

  1. Parallel trends hold
  2. No anticipation (people don't change behavior before treatment)
  3. Homogeneous treatment effects (effect same regardless of when you're treated)

The Risk: With Heterogeneous TEs

TWFE with staggered rollout can be biased if treatment effects vary over time or across units.

Some comparisons use already-treated units as controls (bad!).

Advanced topic: May or may not appear on quiz.

For Exam

If you see staggered rollout: Check assumptions. If effects might be heterogeneous, TWFE might be biased. Alternative: Use only early vs late or use recent methods (Callaway-Sant'Anna).

3J. Seasonality and Other Threats

Is Seasonality a Problem?

Answer: Not if it affects treated and control symmetrically.

Time fixed effects absorb common seasonal patterns.

When Seasonality IS a Problem

If treated and control have systematically different seasonal patterns (e.g., treated region is agricultural, control is urban), then parallel trends might be violated.

Solution: Include time trends or control for seasonal patterns separately.

PART 4: Interpreting Regression Output

Applied Skills

What You'll See on the Exam

A regression table with coefficients, standard errors, and significance stars. You need to:

  • Identify the causal coefficient
  • Interpret in substantive terms
  • Assess significance
  • Spot issues (bad controls, wrong specification, etc.)

Understanding the Standard Regression Output

Component What It Means
Coefficient (β̂) The estimated effect. With controls, it's "holding other variables constant."
Standard Error (SE) Uncertainty in the estimate. Roughly ±2×SE covers 95% confidence interval.
t-stat β̂ / SE. If |t| > 1.96, significant at p<0.05.
p-value Probability of observing this β̂ if true effect is 0. p<0.05 = "significant"
* ** *** Significance stars: * p<0.10, ** p<0.05, *** p<0.01

Example: Log-Level Interpretation (from PS2 Q3)

Model: ln(Priceₘₜ) = β₀ + β₁·PC_Connectₘₜ + β₂·PC_Connect·Electₜ + ...

Results: β̂₁ = 0.05, β̂₂ = -0.03

Interpretation:

  • Politically connected mills have 5% higher prices in non-election years (e^0.05 - 1 ≈ 5%)
  • During elections, the effect decreases by 3 percentage points (β₁ + β₂ = 2% total)

Formula: e^β - 1 ≈ % change (exact for small β, approximation for large)

Multi-Level Specification (from PS2 Q3)

Setup: Data at mill-month level, with mill FE and year FE.

Question: What does β₁ on PC_Connectₘ mean?

Answer: Difference between politically connected and non-connected mills, in non-election years, controlling for mill and year FE. This is a within-mill, within-year comparison.

Exam Technique: Reading Complex Tables

  1. Identify the dependent variable
  2. Spot the key coefficient(s) of interest
  3. Check what controls/FE are included
  4. Interpret the coefficient in context (level vs log, with vs without FE)
  5. Comment on significance

PART 5: Practice Quiz (30 Questions)

Exam-Style Problems

How to Use This Section

These 30 questions follow the exam's format: calculations, conceptual, interpretation, and defense questions. Click each question to see the answer. Try solving before looking!

Section A: Calculation Questions (8 questions)

Q1 SDO Decomposition. You observe: E[Y|D=1] = 15, E[Y|D=0] = 10, E[Y(0)|D=1] = 9, E[Y(0)|D=0] = 10. Calculate SDO, ATT, and Selection Bias. ▼

Solution:

  • SDO = 15 - 10 = 5
  • ATT = E[Y(1)|D=1] - E[Y(0)|D=1] = E[Y(1)|D=1] - 9. But E[Y(1)|D=1] = E[Y|D=1] = 15, so ATT = 15 - 9 = 6
  • SB = E[Y(0)|D=1] - E[Y(0)|D=0] = 9 - 10 = -1
  • Check: SDO = ATT + SB → 5 = 6 + (-1) = 5 ✓

Interpretation: The treated group had lower Y(0) than control (SB = -1), but benefited more from treatment (ATT = 6). The observed difference (SDO = 5) reflects both.

Q2 Compliance Types. 80% assigned to treatment (Z=1), 20% to control. Of those assigned treatment, 90% participated. Of those assigned control, 10% participated. Calculate always-takers, never-takers, and compliers. ▼

Solution:

  • P(D=1|Z=1) = 0.90
  • P(D=1|Z=0) = 0.10
  • Always-Takers: P(D=1|Z=0) = 0.10 = 10%
  • Never-Takers: 1 - P(D=1|Z=1) = 1 - 0.90 = 0.10 = 10%
  • Compliers: P(D=1|Z=1) - P(D=1|Z=0) = 0.90 - 0.10 = 0.80 = 80%
  • Check: 10% + 10% + 80% = 100% ✓ (assuming no defiers)

Interpretation: 80% of population are compliers (affected by assignment), 10% always take treatment, 10% never do.

Q3 ITT and LATE. ITT = 3.2, First Stage = 0.80. What is LATE? ▼

Solution:

LATE = ITT / First Stage = 3.2 / 0.80 = 4.0

Interpretation: Being assigned to treatment reduces the treated outcome by 3.2 units on average (ITT). For the 80% who comply, the actual effect is 4.0 units. (Note: If ITT is negative, LATE is more negative.)

Q4 DiD Calculation. Control group: 20 pre-policy, 22 post-policy. Treated group: 25 pre-policy, 30 post-policy. Calculate the DiD effect. ▼

Solution:

  • Control change: 22 - 20 = 2
  • Treated change: 30 - 25 = 5
  • DiD = 5 - 2 = 3

Alternative formula: DiD = (30 - 25) - (22 - 20) = 5 - 2 = 3 ✓

Interpretation: Treatment caused a 3-unit increase in the outcome. Control group's 2-unit increase (common trend) is subtracted out.

Q5 ATE from RCT. Regression: Y = 50 + 3*Treat. What is the ATE? What's the control group mean? ▼

Solution:

  • ATE = 3 (coefficient on Treat, unbiased under randomization)
  • Control mean = 50 (intercept β₀, since β₀ = E[Y|D=0])
  • Treated mean = 50 + 3 = 53
Q6 Log-Level Elasticity. Ln(Wage) = 2 + 0.08*Education. Interpret the coefficient. ▼

Solution:

Each additional year of education increases wage by approximately 8%.

Formula: e^0.08 - 1 ≈ 0.0833 ≈ 8.33% (more precise)

Approximation: 0.08 ≈ 8% (used for small coefficients)

Q7 Regression with controls. Y = 10 + 2*Treat - 0.5*Age + e. If Treat = 1, Age = 30, what's the predicted Y? ▼

Solution:

Ŷ = 10 + 2(1) - 0.5(30) = 10 + 2 - 15 = -3

Interpretation: For a 30-year-old person assigned treatment, predicted outcome is -3. (In practice, you'd need to interpret this in context—could be negative if outcome is, e.g., unemployment duration.)

Q8 ATT vs ATE. You run a non-randomized study. ATT = 10, p=0.6. What can you say about ATE? ▼

Solution:

ATE = p*ATT + (1-p)*ATU = 0.6*10 + 0.4*ATU = 6 + 0.4*ATU

Without knowing ATU, you cannot determine ATE.

Special case: If ATU = ATT (homogeneous effects), then ATE = 0.6*10 + 0.4*10 = 10 = ATT.

General point: ATT alone doesn't determine ATE; need to know ATU or assume homogeneity.

Section B: Conceptual Questions (8 questions)

Q9 What does the Independence assumption (Y(1),Y(0) ⊥⊥ D) imply for the relationship between SDO and ATE? ▼

Answer: Under independence, E[Y(0)|D=1] = E[Y(0)|D=0], which means Selection Bias = 0. Therefore, SDO = ATE (unbiased).

Intuition: If treatment is independent of potential outcomes, the treated and control groups have the same expected Y(0), so any observed difference is due to the treatment, not selection.

Q10 Explain the difference between ITT and LATE. When would you report each? ▼

ITT: Effect of being assigned treatment (uses Z). Includes everyone assigned, whether they complied or not.

LATE: Effect of actually receiving treatment, for compliers only (uses D adjusted for Z). LATE = ITT / First Stage.

When to use:

  • ITT: If you want the policy-relevant effect (offering the program, people choose whether to participate)
  • LATE: If you want the treatment effect for those who actually take it
Q11 What is SUTVA and why does it matter? Give an example of a violation. ▼

SUTVA: Stable Unit Treatment Value Assumption. Has two parts:

  1. No interference: One person's treatment doesn't affect another's outcome
  2. Single version of treatment: Treatment means the same thing for everyone

Why it matters: If SUTVA is violated, causal effects are biased/misspecified.

Example violation: Vaccine study where vaccinated people create herd immunity for unvaccinated neighbors. One person's treatment (vaccination) affects neighbors' outcomes.

Q12 What is the Parallel Trends assumption in DiD? Why can you not test it post-treatment? ▼

Parallel Trends: The treated and control groups would have followed the same trend absent treatment.

Why can't test post-treatment: After treatment, the treated group's outcome is affected by treatment. You can't observe what would have happened without treatment, so you can't directly check if trends match.

What you CAN do: Test pre-treatment trends. If they're similar before treatment, it's suggestive evidence (though not definitive) that trends would remain parallel post-treatment.

Q13 What is an "Event Study" and what does it show that a simple DiD doesn't? ▼

Event Study: Estimates separate treatment effects for each time period relative to a reference period.

What it shows that DiD doesn't:

  • Dynamic effects (does effect grow, fade, stay constant over time?)
  • Pre-treatment trends (can check if parallel trends assumption reasonable)
  • When effect kicks in (immediate vs delayed)

Trade-off: Requires more periods of data, but more informative.

Q14 Explain why adding pre-treatment control variables reduces standard errors but randomization alone doesn't guarantee this. ▼

Why controls reduce SE: Controls explain variation in the outcome not caused by treatment. This reduces Var(ε) → smaller SE → more precision.

Why randomization alone doesn't guarantee smaller SE: Randomization makes the estimate unbiased (SDO = ATE), but doesn't reduce variance of the error term. Controls do that.

Summary: Randomization = unbiased. Controls = more precise. Both are good.

Q15 What is a "bad control" and why should you avoid it in RCTs? ▼

Bad control: A variable measured AFTER treatment that might be affected by treatment.

Why avoid: If you control for a post-treatment variable that's affected by treatment, you're controlling away part of the treatment effect. This makes the treatment coefficient smaller and biases it downward.

Example: Treatment = tutoring. Bad control = hours studied (tutoring likely increases study hours). With this control, you'd underestimate tutoring's benefit.

Q16 The four LATE assumptions—which one would be violated if the instrument (assignment Z) is correlated with an unobserved confounder of D and Y? ▼

Answer: This would violate the Exclusion Restriction (Assumption 2).

If Z is correlated with an unobserved confounder U that affects both D and Y, then Z affects Y through U independently of D, violating the requirement that Z → Y only through D.

Section C: Interpretation Questions (8 questions)

Q17 Regression output: Y = 100 + 5*Treat*** + 2*Control_Var (SE=1.5). Interpret all three numbers. ▼

Interpretation:

  • 100: Baseline/control group mean (E[Y|Treat=0, Control_Var=0])
  • 5 with ***: Treatment increases Y by 5 units, statistically significant at p<0.01
  • 2 (SE=1.5): Each unit of Control_Var increases Y by 2 units. SE is large relative to coefficient (t≈1.3), likely not significant.

The control variable:** Doesn't reduce the treatment coefficient (still 5), just adjusts for baseline differences to gain precision.

Q18 Regression: ln(Earnings) = 0.50 + 0.12*College**. Interpret the college coefficient and test result. ▼

Interpretation:

  • 0.12: College graduates earn approximately 12% more than non-graduates
  • e^0.12 - 1 ≈ 0.127 ≈ 12.7% (more precise)
  • **: Statistically significant at p<0.05

Important caveat: This is observational, so likely includes selection bias (smarter/more motivated people attend college AND earn more for reasons unrelated to college).

Q19 DiD regression output: β₁ = 4.2** (Treat × Post), with province FE and year FE included. What is this estimate and what does it mean? ▼

What it is: ATT (Average Treatment effect on Treated) for the post-policy period.

What it means: Provinces that implemented the policy had outcomes 4.2 units higher post-policy compared to what they would have experienced following the control group's trend, controlling for province and year fixed effects.

Significance: Significant at p<0.05, so the effect is unlikely due to chance alone.

Q20 Event study output shows: β_{t=-2} = -0.5, β_{t=-1} = 0 (omitted), β_{t=0} = 2.1*, β_{t=1} = 3.0**, β_{t=2} = 3.2**. Interpret the pattern. ▼

Interpretation:

  • t=-2: -0.5, not significant. Slight pre-treatment difference (concerning for parallel trends, but small)
  • t=-1: Omitted reference period (normalized to 0)
  • t=0, 1, 2: Post-treatment effects increasing: 2.1 → 3.0 → 3.2, all significant

Conclusion: Treatment effect emerges gradually and grows over time. Pre-treatment coefficient is close to zero, supporting parallel trends.

Q21 You see: "Regression includes unit and time FE. Treatment coefficient β̂ = 1.5." Why don't the treatment indicator or time indicator appear separately in the regression? ▼

Answer: They're absorbed by the fixed effects.

Why: Treatment indicator only varies by unit (same within unit over time), so unit FE absorbs it. Time indicator only varies by time (same across units), so time FE absorbs it.

The interaction (Treatment × Time) is not absorbed, so it's estimated as β̂ = 1.5.

Q22 Balance table shows: Mean Age (Control) = 42, Mean Age (Treatment) = 45, t-stat = 1.8, p-value = 0.12. What does this tell you about the randomization? ▼

Interpretation:

Treatment and control groups differ in age by 3 years on average (45 vs 42). But this difference is NOT statistically significant (p=0.12 > 0.05).

Conclusion: This could be due to chance (randomization can create small imbalances). Randomization appears to have worked for age.

General note: Expect ~5% of characteristics to be significantly different by chance. If many characteristics have p<0.05, something may have gone wrong with randomization.

Q23 2SLS regression. First stage: D = 0.5 + 0.8*Z** (SE=0.3). What does the first stage tell you? ▼

What it tells you: Assignment (Z) strongly predicts actual treatment (D).

Magnitude: Being assigned to treatment (Z=1 vs Z=0) increases the probability of treatment by 0.8 (or likelihood on margin).

Strength: t-stat = 0.8 / 0.3 = 2.67, significant at p<0.05. Strong instrument.

Check: Coefficient is large relative to SE (F-stat ≈ 7, which is > 10 rule of thumb just barely fails but is decent)

Q24 ITT regression: Y = 10 - 2*Assigned** (SE=0.8), with 1000 observations. Calculate t-stat, p-value (approx), and 95% CI. ▼

Calculations:

  • t-stat: -2 / 0.8 = -2.5
  • p-value: |t| = 2.5 corresponds to p ≈ 0.012 (close to p<0.05)
  • 95% CI: -2 ± 1.96 × 0.8 = [-3.57, -0.43]

Interpretation: Assigning to treatment reduces the outcome by 2 units on average. This effect is statistically significant at p<0.05. We're 95% confident the true effect is between -3.57 and -0.43.

Section D: Defend/Critique Questions (6 questions)

Q25 A researcher reports: "We compared healthcare outcomes for people who got treatment vs those who didn't. Treated group had worse health. We conclude treatment is ineffective." Is this causal? Why or why not? ▼

No, this is not causal. Selection bias!

Why: People in worse health are more likely to seek treatment. The observed worse outcomes in treated group could be because:

  • They were already sicker (Y(0) lower)
  • NOT because treatment caused them to be sicker

SDO overestimates (or is negative while true effect might be positive).

To fix: Use RCT, IV, DiD, or other causal method. Or at minimum, control for severity of illness at baseline.

Q26 A paper uses DiD with treated = early-adopting states, control = late-adopting states. They find a large effect of the policy. Critique this design. ▼

Main critique: Parallel trends assumption is likely violated.

Why: States that adopt early might be systematically different from late-adopters (e.g., more progressive, faster-growing, healthier population). These differences could cause outcomes to trend differently anyway.

Example: Progressive states adopt policy early AND have other policies that improve outcomes. Outcomes rise in early-adopter states for reasons unrelated to the policy.

To strengthen:

  • Test pre-trends formally (F-test)
  • Add robustness checks (falsification tests, alternate control groups)
  • Control for state characteristics that might drive differential trends
Q27 An RCT finds ATE = 8 for a job training program. The researcher says "This effect applies equally to all subgroups." How would you challenge this? ▼

Challenge: RCTs estimate ATE, but don't require homogeneity. Treatment effects can be heterogeneous.

How to check:

  • Estimate effects separately by subgroup (gender, age, education)
  • Interact treatment with subgroup indicators: Y = β₀ + β₁*Treat + β₂*Male + β₃*Treat*Male + ...
  • Test if effects differ: H₀: β₁ = β₁ + β₃ (effect for male = effect for female)

Likely finding: Effects probably do differ. Job training may benefit some groups more than others (e.g., younger people who have more working years left).

Q28 An IV study estimates LATE = 10. The researcher claims "This is the average effect of treatment for the entire population." Do you agree? ▼

No, strongly disagree!

What LATE is: Effect of treatment for COMPLIERS only (those affected by the instrument).

Who are compliers: People who take treatment when assigned but don't when not assigned. This is often a specific subgroup.

Example: In job training study, compliers might be people who are on the margin (would consider training). Always-takers and never-takers are excluded.

Correct claim: "This is the effect for compliers, which is [X]% of the population."

Q29 A researcher includes a post-treatment variable as a control in an RCT regression. They argue "This captures the mechanism by which treatment works." Good idea or bad control? ▼

Bad control! This is post-treatment conditioning.

The problem: If the post-treatment variable is affected by treatment, controlling for it removes part of the treatment effect.

Example: Treatment = tutoring, post-treatment variable = confidence. Tutoring increases confidence, which increases test scores. Controlling for confidence makes the tutoring coefficient smaller (biased down).

Better approach: If interested in mechanism, use mediation analysis (decompose total effect into direct + indirect paths). But don't just control for the mediator in a standard regression.

Q30 A DiD study examines the effect of a minimum wage policy. Pre-trends are flat (no significant pre-policy differences between treated and control counties). The researcher concludes "Parallel trends assumption definitely holds." Do you agree? ▼

Partial agreement with caveat:

What we know: Flat pre-trends is good evidence for parallel trends, but not definitive proof.

Why not definitive: Even with flat pre-trends, trends could diverge post-treatment for reasons unrelated to policy. Example: Economic recession hits treated counties harder (independent of policy effect).

What I'd recommend:

  • Flat pre-trends is good sign. Report it.
  • But also do robustness checks (falsification tests, event study)
  • State assumption cautiously: "Pre-trends are parallel. While post-trends cannot be directly tested, we have no evidence against parallel trends assumption."

Concept Formula Interpretation

Concept Formula Interpretation
